I love this question because this is where products usually succeed or fail.

The biggest mistake would be trying to build **everything** we brainstormed. That would take months before anyone could use it.

Instead, let's think like a startup.

Every phase should answer **one question**.

---

# 🎯 North Star

> Build a platform that lets anyone discover, follow, learn from, and eventually participate in a software engineering journey from **$0 → $1,000,000**.

Everything we build should serve one of these goals:

* Inspire
* Educate
* Build trust
* Build community
* Create sustainability

Nothing else.

---

# The Product Evolution

The website should evolve almost like a person growing up.

Not "features."

**Stages of maturity.**

---

# 🌱 Stage 1 — Existence

Question answered:

> **Who are you?**

The website is basically

> "Hi, I'm building to $1,000,000."

Very simple.

People should immediately understand

* mission
* current goal
* current project
* latest stream

Nothing more.

At this point visitors shouldn't need to click five pages.

---

Deliverables

* Landing page
* About
* Current project
* Current progress
* Latest stream
* Links

This can be finished very quickly.

---

# 🚧 Stage 2 — Credibility

Question answered:

> **Are you actually building?**

Now we prove consistency.

Instead of saying

"I'm building"

we show

"I built."

Introduce

* Ongoing projects
* Completed projects
* Progress tracker
* Project pages
* Stream archive

This is where people begin trusting the journey.

---

# 📖 Stage 3 — Storytelling

Question answered:

> **Can I follow along?**

Now people become emotionally invested.

Introduce

* Timeline
* Daily logs
* Weekly summaries
* Milestones
* Lessons learned

This is no longer documentation.

It's a story.

---

# 🧠 Stage 4 — Education

Question answered:

> **Can I learn from this?**

Only now introduce educational content.

Examples

* Architecture decisions
* Backend notes
* AI notes
* System Design
* Responsible AI
* Engineering articles

At this point every project becomes educational.

---

# 🤝 Stage 5 — Community

Question answered:

> **Can I participate?**

Now visitors become contributors.

Examples

* Comments
* Discussions
* Feature requests
* Project voting
* Public roadmap

People begin shaping the journey.

---

# ❤️ Stage 6 — Support

Question answered:

> **How can I help?**

Notice this comes AFTER community.

People support creators they already trust.

Introduce

* Join the Journey
* Monthly supporters
* Hall of Builders
* Thank-you wall
* Early access

Support should feel like joining the mission, not buying access.

---

# 📊 Stage 7 — Transparency

Question answered:

> **How is the journey going?**

This becomes addictive.

Metrics.

* Revenue
* Subscribers
* Projects
* Streams
* Commits
* Days
* Hours
* Milestones

Everything public.

---

# 🚀 Stage 8 — Ecosystem

Question answered:

> **Can this become bigger than one person?**

Now the website evolves beyond your personal journey.

Introduce

* Guest builders
* Community showcases
* Open-source contributors
* Collaborations
* Challenges
* Hackathons

The mission becomes larger than you.

---

# 🌍 Stage 9 — Platform

Question answered:

> **Can others build their own journey here?**

This is years away.

Imagine anyone creating

> Building to my first SaaS

or

> Learning Rust

or

> 365 Days of AI

using the same platform.

At this point you've built an ecosystem rather than a personal website.

One idea I think we should add that wasn't in our earlier discussion is this:

The website should always feel **alive**.

Every day, something changes.

Maybe it's:

* A completed milestone
* Today's learning
* Yesterday's stream

## One guiding principle I'd like us to adopt

For every feature we consider, we should ask one question:

> **Does this either improve the quality of work entering the pipeline or reduce manual effort after it enters?**

That's already a principle we've been following in your other projects, and it fits here too.

For this platform, I'd adapt it slightly:

> **Every release should do one of three things:**
>
> 1. Make the journey easier to follow.
> 2. Make the content more valuable to learn from.
> 3. Reduce the effort required to keep the platform updated.

If a feature doesn't satisfy at least one of those, it probably belongs in a later milestone rather than the next release. I think that principle will help us avoid feature creep while steadily building something that people genuinely enjoy returning to.
